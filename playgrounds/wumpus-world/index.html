↓ Read the explanation

Russell & Norvig's Wumpus World is the textbook example of a logical agent: one whose actions are determined by what it can prove from what it has observed. The world is 4×4. Somewhere in it are three pits, one wumpus, and one gold. The agent starts at (1,1), can only see its current cell, and gets a stench when adjacent to the wumpus and a breeze when adjacent to a pit. Its job is to grab the gold and climb out without dying.

The percept-knowledge-action loop

Each move, the agent senses its percepts (breeze, stench, glitter), updates its knowledge base, then decides what to do. The knowledge update is the core: a cell with no breeze rules out pits in every adjacent cell; a cell with no stench rules out the wumpus in every adjacent cell; if a cell has both pit ruled out AND wumpus ruled out, it's safe. The agent moves only into known-safe cells.

"Maybe deadly" vs "deadly"

The yellow cells in the knowledge map are the most pedagogically important: they're adjacent to a visited cell, but the agent has neither proven them safe nor proven them deadly. A purely logical agent will refuse to enter them. A probabilistic agent (the topic of Russell & Norvig's next chapter) computes how likely each one is to be deadly and sometimes takes the risk. Watch a few worlds where the agent refuses to move: that's logic hitting its limit, and where probability takes over.

"I haven't seen it" is different from "I've proved it's not there." That distinction: the closed-world assumption versus full logical inference: is the central pedagogical payload of this entire AIMA chapter.

How the inference actually runs

For each cell, the agent tracks three flags: pit, wumpus, and safe: each either yes, no, or unknown. The inference loop repeatedly applies three rules until nothing changes:

  • Rule out: If a visited cell had no breeze, every adjacent cell is not-a-pit. Same for no-stench and the wumpus.
  • Unique-candidate inference: If a cell has a breeze and only one adjacent cell hasn't been ruled out as a pit, that cell is the pit. (Same for stench / wumpus.)
  • Safe consolidation: If both pit and wumpus are ruled out for a cell, it's safe.

These three rules are weaker than full propositional resolution, which would require a SAT solver: but they're enough to teach the lesson and they run instantly in JavaScript.

What this simplifies

A "real" logical agent would represent the knowledge base as propositional sentences and use a SAT solver (or a theorem prover) to answer queries. The full AIMA implementation does exactly that, and would handle cases this applet can't (e.g., constraints across three or more adjacent cells). The agent here also doesn't model shooting the wumpus or strategic risk-taking: both are real features of the full AIMA Wumpus environment.

For teachers

Curriculum: Problem Representation & Search; Probability under uncertainty. Based on Russell & Norvig, AIMA Chapter 7. Percept→knowledge→action loop; the difference between "I haven't seen it" and "I've proved it's not there"; why logic alone isn't always enough.

Pre-exploration prompts:

  • You're in a cave. You feel a breeze. What does that tell you? What does it NOT tell you?
  • The agent only knows what it's seen. How could it ever know a cell is safe without visiting it?
  • What's the difference between "the agent knows X" and "X is true"?

Post-exploration prompts:

  • Generate a few new worlds. How often does the agent reach the gold? When it doesn't, what got it stuck: fear, or actual danger?
  • Turn ON teacher view. Find a cell where the agent thinks "possibly deadly" but in fact is safe. Why didn't it figure that out?
  • The agent can't infer "X is a pit" from one breeze alone. What additional percept would let it pin one down?
  • Why does the agent sometimes refuse to move at all?

Common misconceptions defeated: "the agent sees the whole maze" (no: only its current cell + adjacent percepts); "if it's unsafe, the agent will die" (no: the agent NEVER moves into a known-deadly cell, but may die from an unknown one); "logic is enough" (no: when forced to choose between two "possibly deadly" cells, the agent has to gamble; that's why real AI needs probability).
