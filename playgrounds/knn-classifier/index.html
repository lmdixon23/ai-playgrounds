Splits the training points into f equal folds. For each k in 1..30, trains on f−1 folds and validates on the held-out fold; averages accuracy across all f rotations. The k with highest CV accuracy is the right pick: NOT the k that fits the training data best (that's always k=1).

↓ Read the explanation

K-nearest-neighbors is so simple it doesn't really seem like "machine learning." To classify a new point, you find the k closest training points and take a majority vote. That's the whole algorithm. There is no model. There are no parameters. There is no "training" in any meaningful sense: KNN just stores every training point and queries them on demand.

What k Actually Does

At k = 1, every training point gets its own little neighborhood: the region of the plane closer to it than to any other point. The decision boundary hugs every quirk of the data, and any mislabeled training point creates a tiny bubble of wrong predictions around itself.

At k = 25 on a small dataset, the model votes among nearly all the points every time. The boundary smooths out toward a single majority decision for the entire plane.

Small k = jagged boundary, low bias, high variance (overfits noise). Large k = smooth boundary, higher bias, lower variance (under-fits structure). This is the bias-variance trade-off, in one slider.

Where KNN Wins and Where It Breaks

Try the Two moons dataset. KNN handles it gracefully: the curved boundary just appears, with no model to design. Then try Spiral: KNN still works, but you need careful k to keep the boundary clean. Now try Noisy overlap: there's no "correct" boundary because the classes actually mix. With k = 1 the boundary looks confidently wrong; with k = 25 it admits defeat by predicting the majority class for the overlapped region. That's KNN telling you the truth: the data is ambiguous here.

What This Simplifies

Real KNN implementations use space-partitioning structures (k-d trees, ball trees) so that finding the k nearest neighbors takes O(log n) time instead of the naive O(n) this applet uses. Real KNN also typically standardizes features (so "age in years" doesn't dominate "height in meters" just because it has bigger numbers), uses distance metrics other than Euclidean (cosine for text, Mahalanobis for correlated features), and may weight neighbors by inverse distance rather than treating them as equal votes.

For teachers

Curriculum: Supervised Learning: KNN. Classification by similarity; the role of the hyperparameter k; reading a decision boundary; intuition for the bias-variance trade-off.

Pre-exploration prompts:

  • How might a computer classify a point it has never seen?
  • If you had to guess someone's music taste from their friends', would you ask 1 friend or 20? Why?
  • What does it mean for two points to be "similar"?

Post-exploration prompts:

  • Set k = 1, add a misplaced blue dot in the middle of orange. What happens? Why?
  • Drag k from 1 to 25 on Two Moons. At what k does the model stop seeing the moon shape?
  • On Noisy overlap, what happens to the boundary as k grows? Where's the trade-off?

Common misconceptions defeated: "bigger k is always better" (no: high k smooths away real structure); "the model learns something" (KNN has no parameters); "decision boundaries are straight lines" (they're piecewise-Voronoi).
