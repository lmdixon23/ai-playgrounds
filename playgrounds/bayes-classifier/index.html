↓ Read the explanation

Imagine a disease that affects 1 person in 100. There's a test for it that is "99% accurate" in both directions: when you're sick it says positive 99% of the time, and when you're healthy it says negative 99% of the time. You take the test. It comes back positive. What's the probability that you actually have the disease? Most people guess somewhere between 95% and 99%. The real answer is about 50%. That gap is what Bayes' rule is for.

Why intuition fails

Test accuracy and personal probability are not the same number. "99% accurate" describes how the test behaves when you condition on the truth. What you care about is how the test behaves when you condition on its own output. Bayes' rule converts between the two by adding one piece of information: how common the condition is to begin with: the prior.

The applet above is doing nothing fancy. It's just counting people. Of 10,000 people, 100 are sick (1% prior). Of those 100 sick people, 99 test positive (sensitivity). Of the 9,900 healthy people, 5%: that's 495: test positive anyway (false positives). So of the 99 + 495 = 594 people who test positive, only 99 are actually sick. 99 / 594 ≈ 16.7%.

Bayes' rule isn't a math trick. It's literally counting groups. The "trick" is remembering that the false positives from the much larger healthy group can outnumber the true positives from the small sick group.

The two levers

Two factors push the posterior probability around:

  • The prior. Slide the disease prior from 1% down to 0.1% (one in a thousand : about right for a rare cancer screening). Watch the posterior collapse. Diseases that are genuinely rare make positive tests genuinely uninformative.
  • The specificity. Hold the prior at 1%. Now slide specificity from 95% to 99.9%. The posterior shoots up. Reducing false positives matters far more than reducing false negatives when the prior is low.

The spam scenario is the same math

Click "Spam filter" up top. The math is identical. The numbers are different because spam is common (30%-ish prior) and false positives are catastrophic (a real email goes to junk). A spam filter with 95% sensitivity and 99% specificity has a much better posterior than the same numbers applied to a rare disease: because the prior is so much higher.

What this simplifies

Real Bayesian reasoning chains many pieces of evidence (each test result updates the prior for the next). Real diagnostic tests have a continuous range of outcomes, not just positive/negative: and the cutoff for "positive" itself is a knob with its own bias. Real spam filters use thousands of features rather than one binary indicator. But every one of those systems is doing what this applet is doing: counting groups, then renormalizing.

For teachers

Curriculum: Probability & Naïve Bayes. Apply Bayes' rule; distinguish P(A|B) from P(B|A); understand why test accuracy alone isn't enough to interpret a result.

Pre-exploration prompts:

  • If a test is 99% accurate and yours comes back positive, what's the chance you actually have the condition? (Most students guess ~99%.)
  • What's the difference between "P(test positive given sick)" and "P(sick given test positive)"?
  • How could a "great test" still produce mostly wrong-positive results?

Post-exploration prompts:

  • Set prior = 0.1% and sensitivity = specificity = 99%. Did the posterior surprise you? Why?
  • Hold prior at 1%. Crank specificity from 95% → 99.9%. Why does the posterior change so much?
  • Switch to spam filter. The math is identical: what changes is the consequence of a false positive vs a false negative. Discuss.

Common misconceptions defeated: "test accuracy = my chance of being correct" (conflates P(test|truth) with P(truth|test)); "if the test is positive, I'm probably sick" (only true when prior is high or specificity is near-perfect); "Bayes is a math trick" (it's just counting groups of people).
