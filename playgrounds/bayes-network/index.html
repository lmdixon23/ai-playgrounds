↓ Read the explanation

Bayesian networks let you reason about many variables at once. The classic textbook example from Russell & Norvig has five: a burglary (B), an earthquake (E), an alarm that responds to both (A), and two neighbors (John and Mary) who call when they hear it. Five variables, a few conditional probability tables, and you can ask any question — like "given that John called, what's the probability of a burglary?"

What the arrows mean

Each arrow goes from a parent to a child, and the child's probability table conditions on the values of the parents. In this network, the alarm depends on both burglary and earthquake, and each neighbor's call depends on the alarm. The arrows don't strictly mean "causes" — they encode the dependency structure of the joint probability distribution. Two variables with no arrow between them are conditionally independent given their parents.

The "explaining away" effect

Click "John & Mary both call" and watch P(B) jump from 0.1% to about 28%. Two calls is much stronger evidence than one. Now click "Both call + earthquake" — P(B) collapses back down to about 0.4%. Why? The earthquake "explains away" the alarm. Once you know there's an earthquake, the alarm is no longer surprising, and the burglary is no longer needed as an explanation.

Explaining away is genuinely counterintuitive. Two causes that are statistically independent in the absence of evidence become anti-correlated once you observe their common effect. Burglary and earthquake are independent — until you hear the alarm.

Exact inference vs Gibbs sampling

The "exact" mode computes the posterior by summing over every possible value of every unobserved variable. With 5 variables and one observed, that's 2⁴ = 16 sums. Fine. With 100 variables it's 2⁹⁹ — not fine. Gibbs sampling instead generates a sequence of states by repeatedly resampling one variable at a time conditional on the others. Crank the samples slider from 1,000 to 50,000 and watch the noise narrow. The estimates converge to the exact answer in the limit — but only in the limit.

What this simplifies

Real Bayesian networks have hundreds or thousands of variables and learned CPTs (you don't write them by hand — you fit them from data). Real inference uses much smarter methods than naïve enumeration or vanilla Gibbs: variable elimination, junction trees, loopy belief propagation, variational inference. The heart, though, is what you see here — the rules of conditional probability applied to a graph.

For teachers

Curriculum: Probability beyond Bayes' rule. Russell & Norvig Chapter 14. Directed graphical models; conditional probability tables; "explaining away" as a structural phenomenon; exact vs. sampling inference.

Pre-exploration prompts:

  • If your alarm goes off, what's the probability there's actually a burglar?
  • If you learn an earthquake happened, does that make you LESS worried about a burglary? Why?
  • What's the difference between an event being "more likely" and "more probable"?

Post-exploration prompts:

  • Click "John calls." P(Burglary) rises a lot — but not to 100%. Why or why not?
  • Click "John & Mary both call." Why does TWO calls move the needle so much more?
  • Click "Both call + earthquake." Watch P(Burglary) collapse. This is "explaining away" live.
  • Switch to Gibbs at 1,000 samples and re-sample a few times. Watch the wobble. Then crank to 50,000 and re-sample. What does this say about sampling-based AI?
  • Edit P(B) from 0.001 to 0.1 in the CPT. Does it change your conclusions in the John-calls case?

Common misconceptions defeated: "Bayes nets just encode causal arrows" (no — arrows encode conditional independence; causation is an interpretation); "evidence can only make a hypothesis MORE likely" (no — explaining away is a real anti-correlation); "Gibbs is just exact inference but slower" (no — it's an approximation, never exact).
