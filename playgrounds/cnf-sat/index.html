↓ Read the explanation

Propositional logic is the simplest form of logic that does anything interesting. You have variables (each either true or false), the four classical connectives (not, and, or, implies, iff), and a question: given a bag of sentences, is there any assignment of true/false to the variables that makes all of them simultaneously true? That question is called SAT. This applet walks you through how a computer answers it.

Why CNF first

A formula in Conjunctive Normal Form is an AND of ORs of literals (variables or their negations). Every propositional formula can be rewritten into CNF, and every SAT solver in production expects CNF as input. The reason: CNF has just enough structure that powerful algorithms (DPLL, CDCL, watched literals) can exploit it efficiently. The conversion is mechanical — five rewriting rules applied in order.

The five-step rewrite

  1. Eliminate ↔: A ↔ B becomes (A → B) ∧ (B → A).
  2. Eliminate →: A → B becomes ¬A ∨ B.
  3. Push ¬ inward (De Morgan): ¬(A ∧ B) becomes ¬A ∨ ¬B; ¬(A ∨ B) becomes ¬A ∧ ¬B; ¬¬A becomes A.
  4. Distribute ∨ over ∧: A ∨ (B ∧ C) becomes (A ∨ B) ∧ (A ∨ C).
  5. Flatten into a list of clauses (each clause is a disjunction of literals).
Step 4 (distribute ∨ over ∧) is where formula size can explode. (A ∧ B) ∨ (C ∧ D) becomes four clauses; a chain of n such conjunctions can produce 2n clauses. That's why iff-heavy formulas — like the Wumpus pit rule B11 ↔ (P12 ∨ P21) — look so big after conversion.

Why brute force is enough here, but never enough in general

With n variables there are 2n possible truth assignments. The applet enumerates all of them (capped at n = 12, so 4,096 rows). With 30 variables that becomes a billion rows — still doable but slow. With 100, it's 1030, which is more rows than there are atoms in your laptop. SAT is the canonical NP-complete problem; real solvers use clever search (DPLL with unit propagation and conflict-driven clause learning) to prune the search space dramatically. Modern SAT solvers routinely handle problems with millions of variables that brute force can't touch.

The Wumpus example is the bridge to Chapter 7

Click "Wumpus pit rule" up top. It encodes B11 ↔ (P12 ∨ P21): "breeze at (1,1) iff there's a pit at (1,2) or (2,1)." This is exactly the kind of sentence a Wumpus-world agent builds in its knowledge base. The whole point of the agent (see the Wumpus World applet) is to answer SAT queries against many such sentences to decide where it's safe to move.

What this simplifies

Real SAT solvers use DPLL with watched literals, conflict-driven clause learning, restarts, and variable activity heuristics. Real-world SAT problems often have 10⁶+ variables and 10⁷+ clauses. Real propositional reasoning extends to first-order logic (variables that range over objects, not just true/false), where SAT becomes the undecidable problem of theorem-proving. The five rewrite rules and brute-force enumeration here are the educational entry point, not the production tool.

For teachers

Curriculum: Problem Representation (logical knowledge base). Propositional syntax; CNF as a normal form; SAT as the canonical NP-complete decision problem; model counting; reading a truth assignment.

Pre-exploration prompts:

  • If two sentences are equivalent, are they identical? What's the difference?
  • What's the difference between "P → Q" and "Q → P"? Can you convince a computer of the difference?
  • If three friends each refuse to be at the party with one specific other person, is there any assignment that satisfies all of them?

Post-exploration prompts:

  • Load "Modus ponens." Why is Q forced?
  • Load "XOR UNSAT." Why is the formula unsatisfiable? Trace through every assignment.
  • Load "Wumpus pit rule." Look at the CNF expansion of B11 ↔ (P12 ∨ P21). Why does ↔ explode into so many clauses?
  • Add a sentence to a SAT formula that's a contradiction. Does the model count drop to zero?
  • Why do we convert to CNF at all? (Hint: every SAT solver in the world expects CNF as input.)

Common misconceptions defeated: "SAT is easy" (it's NP-complete; brute force is 2n); "all formulas have a unique CNF" (no — they have many equivalent CNF forms; rewrite order matters); "logic and probability are unrelated" (no — probability is what you do when logic alone leaves too many satisfying models).
