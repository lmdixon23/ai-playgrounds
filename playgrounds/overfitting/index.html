Fixes the current degree, λ, regularizer, and noise; varies n from 5 → n_max. For each n, draws a fresh sample of n training points and computes train and test MSE. Plots both curves. Diagnostic: if train ≈ test and both high → underfitting (more capacity); if train low + test high (gap) → overfitting (more data or regularization); if both converging low → good.

↓ Read the explanation

The single most important idea in machine learning is also the simplest: a model that does well on the data you trained it on isn't necessarily a good model. The data that matters is the data you haven't seen. This applet makes that distinction visible in two charts.

Polynomial fitting as the laboratory

The applet samples points from a true function (sin, cubic, step, or linear), adds Gaussian noise, then fits a polynomial of the degree you choose to those noisy points. Degree 1 is a straight line. Degree 20 has 20 parameters: enough to weave a curve through nearly any 20 points. The left chart shows the noisy training points (blue), the true function (dashed grey), and the fitted polynomial (pink). The right chart shows MSE vs degree, with both train and test error.

Why train error keeps falling forever

Any time you give a model more parameters, it can drive its training error down (or at worst keep it the same). At degree n−1 with n training points, the polynomial passes exactly through every point: train error literally zero. This is great news if your only goal is to memorize the training set, and useless news if you want to predict anything new.

Why test error has a sweet spot

The test error tells a different story. At low degrees the model is too rigid to capture the true shape: that's bias. At high degrees the model fits the noise as well as the signal: that's variance. Somewhere in the middle is the sweet spot, visible as the minimum of the orange test-error curve.

The U-shape of the test-error curve is the canonical picture of the bias-variance trade-off. Train error monotonically decreases; test error first decreases (bias falling) then increases (variance rising). The minimum is where you actually want to be.

What regularization does

Ridge regression adds λ‖w‖² to the loss, penalizing large coefficients. Set degree to 20 and watch the wild oscillating fit. Now slide λ up. The coefficients shrink, the fit smooths out, and test error drops: even though the model still has 20 parameters. This is why "more parameters" isn't always the right framing. What matters is the effective capacity, which regularization controls.

Why noise = 0 makes overfitting impossible

Slide σ to 0. Both train and test error become identical at every degree: the test error monotonically falls along with train error. With no noise, there's nothing to overfit. Overfitting is the model treating noise as signal; remove the noise and the problem evaporates. Real data always has noise, which is why this matters in practice.

What this simplifies

Real ML uses cross-validation (not a single held-out test set) to estimate test error reliably. Real models have many regularization knobs (L1, L2, dropout, early stopping, weight decay, data augmentation), not just one λ. Real overfitting in deep learning is more subtle: modern networks have millions of parameters but don't always overfit, a phenomenon called "double descent" that the polynomial picture doesn't capture. But the basic intuition: train error is a liar, test error is what you want: is universal.

For teachers

Curriculum: Evaluation, Leakage, and Regularization. Distinguish train vs test error; identify under- and over-fitting; understand how model complexity, dataset size, and regularization interact.

Pre-exploration prompts:

  • If a model gets 100% on the data it studied, does that mean it's a good model?
  • Why is it cheating to use the same data to train AND evaluate a model?
  • What does "the model memorized the noise" mean?

Post-exploration prompts:

  • At degree 1, both train and test error are high. Why is that called "underfitting"?
  • Slide degree from 1 to 20. Train error always goes down. Test error has a minimum, then climbs. Why?
  • Set degree=15 and watch test error. Now slide λ from 0 to 5. What does regularization "do" to a flexible model?
  • Set noise to 0. Now overfitting can't happen: why?

Common misconceptions defeated: "lower training error = better model" (it's the test error that matters); "regularization makes a model worse" (it can make a flexible model BETTER on new data); "more parameters always overfit" (only if the data is small/noisy: try n=80).
